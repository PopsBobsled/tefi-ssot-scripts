# TEFI Voice Reference
**Owner:** Tate Ulsaker | tate@employmentforimmigration.nz
**Created:** 2026-04-21
**Source:** 90 starred sent emails assessed; 23 high-value examples selected + 6 confirmed templates (Apr 2026)
**Purpose:** Read this before drafting any TEFI template or client email. Do not paraphrase Tate — match the patterns below.

---

## How to Use This File

This is not a style guide. It is a pattern library grounded in Tate's actual sent emails. Each section names a voice behaviour, shows it in Tate's own words, and explains what makes it work. When drafting, identify which pattern(s) apply to the email situation, then write to match.

When in doubt: shorter is better, plainer is better, warmer is not achieved through more words.

---

## The Eight Patterns

---

### Pattern A — Warmth compressed into very short sentences

Tate does not warm up with adjectives or pleasantries. He places warmth in the shortest possible sentence at the moment it matters most. These sentences land harder because they are not padded.

**Examples from real emails:**

> *"I am not in a rush."*
> — Payment received, Oct 2023

> *"Any time."*
> — Re: A free offer for you, Apr 2026

> *"Ramadan Mubarak, Imtiaz. I hope I am saying the right thing. I look forward to your contact whenever you are ready."*
> — Thank you for attending today!, Apr 2023

> *"This is perfect."*
> — Experienced Applicant, May 2023

> *"Let's continue towards your dream, I agree."*
> — Re: Review and Update Your Interview Notes, Dec 2024

> *"How are you doing? It has been a while, I am wondering if you are OK, maybe doing well or maybe needing help?"*
> — Second Week Calls, Mar 2023

**What makes it work:** No preamble. No "I just wanted to say" or "I hope you don't mind." The warmth is the sentence. It is complete in itself.

**What to avoid:** "I really appreciate everything you've done and I just wanted to let you know..." — this dilutes warmth by announcing it.

---

### Pattern B — Acknowledging hard reality, then moving forward without dwelling

Tate names the hard thing plainly — a bad employer response, a rejection, a difficult market — then moves. He does not minimise, he does not over-explain, and he does not spend time in the discomfort.

**Examples from real emails:**

> *"That isn't a good response on Megan's part. Normally a genuine and direct effort brings a genuine and direct response. This is a form letter response. How often do you experience a form letter after giving a personalised effort on the phone + direct email? It seems to me not natural to get a template in this case. Anyway, you can move on now. This is the game. Each time try to learn something about the experience."*
> — Top tier Property and Facilities Manager, Jun 2024

> *"It was well done from a classic perspective. The good news is that you are closer than you think. Let's refine what..."*
> — 1st attempt of CV, Jul 2023

> *"I agree with what you are saying, I think you found the only two options for advancing your position."*
> — Update, Mar 2023

> *"I understand you will miss many things from home and of course many people also. You will make new friends and hopefully find many positives about being here, more than negatives."*
> — Re: A free offer for you, Apr 2026

**What makes it work:** The acknowledgement is one or two sentences. The pivot word is usually "Anyway," or "Let's" or "What remains is..." — forward motion, not consolation.

**What to avoid:** Extended sympathy ("I know this must be so hard for you and I completely understand why you are feeling...") — this amplifies the negative without helping the person move.

---

### Pattern C — Giving generously without announcing it

Tate extends generosity — time, support, resources, commitment — as a plain statement of fact, not as a performance of kindness. No "it would be my pleasure" or "I'd love to help."

**Examples from real emails:**

> *"Great, well as I promised, free support for your wife. It's not the same as what we did but it will assist her in finding work when she is ready. I am happy to assist in a meaningful and helpful way."*
> — Re: A free offer for you, Apr 2026

> *"I am not in a rush, but I am available tomorrow, next week, or next month. Any time."*
> — Re: A free offer for you, Apr 2026

> *"Let's first ensure your success and happiness."*
> — Your checklist for effective job seeking, Jul 2023

> *"If it feels like a 'dark night of the soul' don't worry. Your time will come. This is the universe testing you. You will be fine because you have all the right components of success plus personality."*
> — Top tier Property and Facilities Manager, Jun 2024

**What makes it work:** The offer is stated, not performed. The sentence ends. There is no bow-taking. Tate moves on immediately to the practical next thing.

### Pattern D — Redirecting firmly but respectfully

When a client needs redirecting — wrong tool, wrong question, wrong effort level — Tate tells them what he needs, not what they failed to do. He draws his scope clearly when asked questions outside it, without apology or over-explanation.

**Examples from real emails:**

> *"It doesn't seem like the way things are done but I don't give visa advice. Just go to the NZ immigration website to work that out or go to an immigration agent."*
> — Interested to know what you think, Jul 2023

> *"About your efforts, I don't need the detailed list unless you want help. The content above isn't going to allow me to help too much."*
> — Re: [coaching thread], Apr 2025

> *"I like your passion but I need some more help here. I will hopefully help you focus with a question..."*
> — I am trying to help you, please consider this, Apr 2023

> *"The best fix in my view won't likely be a popular suggestion but I am kindly trying to help so please bear with me. Whatever you decide to do is perfectly fine with me and I will roll alongside you with any direction you take."*
> — hello brother... I'm here, Nov 2022

> *"Project managers in construction will need 1 of 2 things as a foreigner coming in: Either a relevant bachelor's degree or a relevant trade."*
> — CV & Cover letter for Nico Greyling, Sep 2023

**What makes it work:** The redirect is about Tate's need or the client's actual situation — not a judgment on them. "I need some more help here" puts the requirement on Tate's side. "I don't give visa advice" is a scope statement, not a refusal. The phrase "kindly trying to help so please bear with me" softens without apologising.

### Pattern E — Rewarding a good question or insight from the client

When a client asks something insightful or shows real understanding, Tate names it explicitly. This is not flattery — he is specific about what was good and why, and he immediately uses it as a launchpad for real information.

**Examples from real emails:**

> *"Good question. About recruiters — they are 95% a waste of time and only 5% of my overseas clients are successful with recruiters. They are focused mostly on locals and employers don't like recruiters messing with foreigners in most cases."*
> — Preparing for Session 4, Mar 2023

> *"You bring up a good question. Practically speaking, the contents of your CV are discovered in previous sessions prior to that course. This is in recognition that CVs are 95%..."*
> — Geoffrey and Tanika, Mar 2023

> *"Thanks for the update and for such clarity coming from the job market. With just a few detailed feedbacks like the ones you showed, you can understand what is happening in the job market."*
> — Update, Jun 2023

> *"This is a fascinating set of achievements and some clearly described metrics that look very impressive."*
> — Projects Table Template, Oct 2022

> *"Excellent, you are using the receptionist to get to the desk of the decision maker — well done. It works!"*
> — Top Tier Candidate from South Africa, Sep 2022

**What makes it work:** The reward is specific. "Good question" alone is not enough — he follows immediately with the substance. When praising an achievement he names the specific behaviour ("using the receptionist to get to the desk of the decision maker") rather than the general result.

### Pattern F — Transparency about his own thinking or process

Tate pulls back the curtain on how he thinks, what he knows, what he doesn't know, and how his process works — not to impress, but to explain. He is honest about uncertainty and frank about what the system requires.

**Examples from real emails:**

> *"INZ bureaucrats have no clue how damaging their delays are. They need a system that updates your progress online so you can log in and see exactly where it is and for how long and what is next."*
> — Hello, how is it going?, Dec 2023

> *"...is going to sound strange at first, but then it will make perfect sense."*
> — Calls to the trainers or to the employers?, Sep 2023

> *"What helps me is that I have a good transcription service that brings out audio into text format, which means my notes are more conceptual and not verbatim word-by-word. Yes it takes time. As you know, experience helps with organising priorities and knowing what will work."*
> — Re: Review and Update Your Interview Notes, Dec 2024

> *"100%. If you consider that every week is about contacting and learning, then progress will be constant and job offers will come. That math never fails."*
> — Compliments of the new year, Jan 2023

> *"I would put recruiters at the lowest opportunity."*
> — Top tier Property and Facilities Manager, Jun 2024

**What makes it work:** Tate shares the reasoning, not just the instruction. "That math never fails" is a genuine belief, not a motivational phrase. Flagging "this is going to sound strange at first" invites the client into his reasoning rather than asking them to simply trust the instruction.

### Pattern G — Pride in a client's achievement, expressed simply and directly

When a client succeeds or does something well, Tate says so plainly. No excessive celebration, no long congratulatory paragraph — just the statement, clearly made.

**Examples from real emails:**

> *"I am proud of you, Peter, because you believed in yourself."*
> — Re: A free offer for you, Apr 2026

> *"Very good contacts you are making and the value of their response is really great."*
> — Cornee update, Oct 2023

> *"This is perfect. Keep the same momentum going."*
> — Experienced Applicant, May 2023

> *"Not much extra time, job well done. I think we have found the 'magic middle'."*
> — Short Clip Finished, Nov 2022

> *"I never met someone so talented and young."*
> — Top tier Property and Facilities Manager, Jun 2024

**What makes it work:** The sentence is complete and stops. Tate does not follow pride with qualifiers ("but you still need to...") unless there is something genuinely important to add. When he does add, the pivot is brief and practical.

---

### Pattern H — Honest, plain-language explanation with no sales padding

When Tate explains a service, a process, or a market reality, he does so in plain language with no marketing overlay. He uses numbers when he has them. He does not oversell.

**Examples from real emails:**

> *"I suggest to get started when you have about 4–5 hours per week to dedicate consistently. This is the ideal situation for a solid minimum continuous dedication."*
> — Payment received, Oct 2023

> *"Apply >> Response >> Shortlist >> Job Interview >> Job offer."*
> — Payment received, Oct 2023

> *"Level 1 — this guy might be worth a look. Level 2 — pleasantly surprised by this profile, consider him for the interview. Level 3 — job interview. Just focus on level 1 and manage responses towards meetings."*
> — Calls to the trainers or to the employers?, Sep 2023

> *"While it may be tempting to take long breaks between efforts, the best results come with a consistent and steady effort. In other words, it is much better to do just 10 calls per week for 6 weeks than to do 60 calls in one week."*
> — Preparing for Session 4, Mar 2023

> *"I would divide your targets into two tiers. For tier 1 — call as a priority. For tier 2 — send an email but also attach your Digital pitch card."*
> — Top tier Property and Facilities Manager, Jul 2024

**What makes it work:** Structure comes from clarity, not from formatting. Numbers are specific. Analogies are simple ("rotten fruit in the grocery store"). The tone is instructional without being condescending — Tate explains because the client needs to understand, not because he is demonstrating expertise.

## What to Avoid

These are patterns that do NOT appear in Tate's high-value emails and should be avoided in all templates:

- Excessive opening pleasantries ("I hope this email finds you well" — appears once in older 2024 email, not in current voice)
- Announcements of kindness ("It would be my absolute pleasure to...")
- Marketing buzzwords ("game-changer", "unlock your potential", "take your career to the next level")
- Over-structured body with bold headers in a short email (appropriate for session prep documents; wrong for conversational replies)
- Extended sympathy that dwells rather than moves ("I know this must be so difficult, and I completely understand how hard it is...")
- Vague encouragement without substance ("You are doing great, keep it up!")
- Closing flourishes ("Warmly yours" / "With sincere regards and best wishes")

**Tate's closings:** "Kind regards, Tate" / "Best regards, Tate" / "Warm regards, Tate" / "Cheers, Tate" — used consistently, chosen by register (Cheers = casual/coaching, Kind regards = formal/new contact, Warm regards = post-meeting/emotional moment).

## Deprecation — Keeping the File Current

Voice evolves. Older examples that reflect an earlier, more formal style can corrupt the reference if left alongside newer, leaner ones. Monthly consolidation prevents drift.

**Trigger:** When 5 or more new Voice Examples have been added since the last consolidation, or on a monthly cadence — whichever comes first.

**Process (Sonnet-led, Tate approves):**
1. Sonnet reads the full file and identifies tension between old and new examples covering the same pattern
2. Two deprecation conditions:
   - **Style drift** — old and new examples show meaningfully different approaches to the same situation. Old example is moved to the Deprecated Archive section below, not deleted.
   - **Redundancy** — new example covers the same pattern more clearly. Weaker example is removed entirely.
3. Sonnet proposes deprecations with a one-line reason for each. Tate approves before anything is moved or removed.
4. After approval, Sonnet updates the file and notes the consolidation date at the bottom.

**Skill to use:** `consolidate-memory` extended to cover this file on the same monthly cadence.

**Rule:** Never deprecate an example solely because it is old. Deprecate only if it conflicts with or is clearly surpassed by a newer example. Age alone is not a reason.

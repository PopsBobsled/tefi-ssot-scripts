# TEFI Communication Principles — Archetypes

**Version:** 1.0 | **Last updated:** 2026-03 | **Owner:** Tate — TEFI

---

## PURPOSE

This document defines the universal communication archetypes that govern all TEFI outputs — emails, reports, proposals, chatbot responses, and consulting dialogue.

These are **principles**, not templates. They apply across channels and adapt to context, but the core signal remains constant.

---

## PRINCIPLE 1: ACKNOWLEDGEMENT-FIRST SEQUENCE

**Archetype:**
Recognition → Context → Benefit → Call to Action

**What it means:**
Begin all client-facing communication by acknowledging something specific and real about their profile, situation, or request. This establishes that you've actually read/understood them.

**Application:**
- Email openings
- Proposal summaries
- Chatbot responses to client queries
- Report introductions

**Example signals:**
- "Your CV is strong — the depth of your IT leadership across Shell and Gazprom-Neft gives me a lot to work with"
- "I can see you've been in roles that required both technical depth and commercial thinking"

**Why it matters:**
Builds credibility and trust. Shows the client they are not being processed through a generic system.

---

## PRINCIPLE 2: BENEFIT-BEFORE-BURDEN FRAMING

**Archetype:**
State what the client gains → Then ask for effort/information

**What it means:**
When requesting information, always explain upfront what that information enables you to deliver. Frame the ask as an investment in their own outcome, not a form to fill.

**Application:**
- High-Value question sections
- Information request emails
- Data gathering in proposals

**Example signals:**
- "With a few more details, the Tier 1 report will position you more precisely in the NZ and AU market"
- "The detail you provide now directly determines how accurate and useful that output is"

**Why it matters:**
Clients are more likely to respond thoughtfully when they understand what the effort unlocks.

---

## PRINCIPLE 3: SPECIFICITY-OVER-GENERALITY

**Archetype:**
Always name the context, the metric, the specific role or achievement.

**What it means:**
Never ask generic questions or make generic statements. Every question must be traceable to something in the client's CV. Every observation must reference a specific role, achievement, or gap.

**Application:**
- Question design in Tier 1 questionnaires
- Profile assessments in snapshots
- Recommendations in reports

**Example signals:**
- ❌ "Tell me about your leadership experience"
- ✅ "In your role at Salym Petroleum, you mention managing a $15M budget and a team of 43 — are there project delivery outcomes you can put a dollar figure on?"

**Why it matters:**
Specificity signals that the consultant has done deep work. It also makes client responses more useful because they're answering a real, contextual question.

---

## PRINCIPLE 4: TONE — WARM, DIRECT, COACH-LIKE

**Archetype:**
Professional structure + Human language

**What it means:**
Match formal document structure with conversational, direct language. No jargon. No corporate filler. No "hardworking professional seeking opportunities."

**Application:**
- All written communication
- Tone register across all channels

**Markers of this tone:**
- Second person ("you/your" not "the candidate")
- Short sentences mixed with medium sentences (rhythm, not uniformity)
- Direct statements without hedging ("Your profile is strong" not "Your profile may be considered to have certain strengths")
- Genuine warmth without false enthusiasm

**Why it matters:**
Distinguishes TEFI from generic career services. Signals respect for the client's intelligence and time.

---

## PRINCIPLE 5: SOURCE-OF-TRUTH DISCIPLINE

**Archetype:**
Never invent. Always reference. Always cite.

**What it means:**
- All claims about the client come from CV or client reply
- All metrics come directly from source or are disclosed aggregations
- All salary/market data come from current NZ/AU knowledge or are disclosed as estimates
- No assumptions beyond what the client has stated

**Application:**
- Every bullet in a report must be traceable to source
- Salary estimates include the word "estimate" and are calibrated to current market norms
- Missing information is flagged as [[MISSING: X]] internally, never invented

**Exception:**
Labour market conditions, salary bands, and role demand data in NZ/AU markets may draw on current model knowledge (not sourced from client materials).

**Why it matters:**
Builds long-term trust. Protects TEFI from being accused of fabrication or overstatement.

## PRINCIPLE 6: QUESTION TAXONOMY — HIGH vs MEDIUM VALUE

**Archetype:**
Separate questions by impact on final output. Ask only what's relevant to their role.

**High-Value gaps** (ask if absent or thin):
- Quantified achievements ($, %, team size, project scope, time saved)
- Scope of responsibility and progression over time
- Key technical skills implied but not explicitly named
- Role target clarity (CIO vs consultant vs manager?)
- Qualification equivalency awareness

**Medium-Value gaps** (ask if absent or thin):
- Sector context (public/private, regulated/unregulated, SME/enterprise)
- Work environment preferences (site, office, remote, travel)
- Target location or geographic flexibility
- Migration timeline
- Ongoing development (courses, certifications in progress)

**Why split?**
Respects client time. High-Value questions directly shape the report. Medium-Value questions add credibility and nuance. Low-Value questions (nice-to-know but not report-shaping) are never asked.

## PRINCIPLE 7: CONFIDENCE WITHOUT OVERSTATEMENT

**Archetype:**
Be genuinely confident in strong profiles. Be honest about constraints.

**What it means:**
- If the profile is strong, say so clearly (no hedging)
- If there are gaps or constraints, name them directly
- If something is uncertain, say "likely" or "estimated" — don't project false certainty
- Offer realistic ceilings for outcomes (5-year trajectory, not fantasy outcomes)

**Application:**
- Profile assessments
- Income estimates
- Role fit commentary
- Career trajectory framing

**Why it matters:**
Builds credibility. Clients respect honesty more than inflated optimism. It also protects TEFI's reputation when outcomes don't match overblown promises.

---

## PRINCIPLE 8: INVITATION-BASED ENGAGEMENT

**Archetype:**
Always offer a next step, but make it optional and low-pressure.

**What it means:**
- End emails with a genuine invite to discuss, but don't push
- Offer multiple channels (Calendly, email reply, phone)
- Frame the next step as "when you're ready" not "you must"
- Respect the client's pace

**Application:**
- Email closings
- Report conclusions
- Proposal endings

**Why it matters:**
Signals confidence in the work. Clients who self-select for deeper engagement tend to be more engaged throughout.
